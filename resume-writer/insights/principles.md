# Resume Writer Insights Library

Curated, attributed insights from recruiters, hiring managers, HR professionals, resume experts, and other experienced hiring voices. Every principle here has a named source. For universal structural hygiene that applies regardless of source — section order, length, formatting, bullet mechanics, typography, file format, and red flags — see `references/common-patterns.md`.

## Precedence rule

During an audit, a violation might map to both a library principle and a common pattern. When both apply, **cite the library principle**. Library principles carry a named source and specific framing, which makes the audit more credible to the reader. Patterns are the fallback.

## Lead with project impact, not credentials

Rule: Do not open a resume with a stack of certifications. Open with the most impactful project or outcome you have delivered. Certifications belong further down the document.

Rationale: Hiring managers care about what you have done with your skills, not whether you passed a test. Certifications are a baseline filter, not a differentiator. Leading with them signals that credentials are your strongest asset, which is a weak opening.

Example violation: A ServiceNow architect resume that leads with "CSA Certified, CAD Certified, CIS-HR Certified" before any project history.

Example fix: Lead the resume with a headline bullet like "Migrated 200 catalog items in 6 weeks during Fortune 500 ServiceNow consolidation, retiring 4 legacy tools."

Sources:

Brandon Wilson, CTA, MMIS, Founder of OnlyFlows and ServiceNow Practice Lead at Four Dragons — LinkedIn, April 2026

## Every bullet must name the thing

Rule: Generic bullets like "configured modules" or "improved processes" are worthless. Every bullet must name the specific module, system, process, artifact, or environment involved.

Rationale: Vague bullets prevent the recruiter from evaluating actual scope and technical depth. If you configured modules, say which ones. If you improved a process, name the process. Without specifics, the bullet tells the reader nothing.

Example violation: "Configured ServiceNow modules to support business operations."

Example fix: "Configured ServiceNow ITSM, HR Service Delivery, and CSM modules, including HRSD workflows for onboarding automation that replaced a 4 step manual process."

Sources:

Brandon Wilson, CTA, MMIS, Founder of OnlyFlows and ServiceNow Practice Lead at Four Dragons — LinkedIn, April 2026

## Numbers, everywhere, or you get an interview with nobody

Rule: Every bullet that describes an outcome should quantify it where the data exists. Use dollars, percentages, time saved, volume handled, people managed, tickets resolved, latency reduced, or other measurable proof.

Rationale: "Improved incident response" is unverifiable and forgettable. Quantified outcomes make scale and business value obvious fast.

Example violation: "Improved incident response times and reduced ticket backlog."

Example fix: "Reduced MTTR from 4.2 hours to 1.8 hours across 12,000 monthly incidents, cutting ticket backlog from 1,800 to under 200 within 90 days."

Sources:

Brandon Wilson, CTA, MMIS, Founder of OnlyFlows and ServiceNow Practice Lead at Four Dragons — LinkedIn, April 2026

## Two pages is the ceiling

Rule: Treat resume length as a prioritization test. For most professional candidates, two pages is the ceiling. If you cannot fit your case into two pages, you have not finished editing.

Rationale: Recruiters do not reward completeness. They reward relevance. Overlong resumes signal weak prioritization and make strong evidence harder to find.

Example violation: A 3.5 page resume with 11 bullets under a role from 2014 and a full paragraph listing every tool ever touched.

Example fix: Reduce the document to two pages by collapsing older roles, cutting redundant bullets, grouping skills, and keeping only evidence that supports the current target role.

Sources:

Brandon Wilson, CTA, MMIS, Founder of OnlyFlows and ServiceNow Practice Lead at Four Dragons — LinkedIn, April 2026
Heather Krasna, Author of The Muse guide for HR resumes — The Muse, October 13, 2021 (adds: one or two pages is enough when the resume is formatted cleanly and focused)

## A resume is a sales document, not a job history

Rule: A resume should persuade the reader to interview you. It is not a chronological dump of every task you have ever performed. Every line must earn its place by making the case that you should be hired.

Rationale: Job histories bore recruiters. Sales documents compel them. A resume that reads like a copied job description for your past roles has failed.

Example violation: "Responsible for managing incident response team and ensuring SLA compliance."

Example fix: "Led a 7 person incident response team to 99.2% SLA compliance across 12,000 monthly tickets, up from 87% at start of tenure."

Sources:

Brandon Wilson, CTA, MMIS, Founder of OnlyFlows and ServiceNow Practice Lead at Four Dragons — LinkedIn, April 2026

## Make the target role obvious in the top third

Rule: The top third of the resume must tell the reader exactly what role you want and why you fit it. Do not make the recruiter infer your direction from scattered clues.

Rationale: Recruiters skim fast. If the target role is not obvious immediately, they will often move on instead of decoding the candidate.

Example violation: A resume that starts with a name, contact info, and a vague summary like "Experienced professional with a diverse background."

Example fix: Add a clear professional title and summary such as "Senior Financial Planning and Analysis Professional" followed by 2 to 3 lines showing domain strength, scope, and key wins.

Sources:

Amanda Augustine, Career Advice Expert — TopResume, December 13, 2015
Jane Heifetz, Founder and Principal of Right Résumés — Harvard Business Review, July 28, 2015 (adds: the summary should be tailored to the role, not generic)

## The summary must be targeted, not decorative

Rule: If you include a summary, tailor it to the specific role or role family you are pursuing. A summary is not an objective statement and not a place for vague adjectives.

Rationale: A good summary helps the recruiter understand fit in seconds. A generic summary wastes premium space and signals that the resume was sprayed everywhere unchanged.

Example violation: "Seeking a challenging opportunity where I can leverage my skills and grow professionally."

Example fix: "Infrastructure automation engineer with 10 years building PowerShell, Azure, and API driven workflows in enterprise environments. Best suited for platform engineering, cloud automation, and DevOps roles that need hands on delivery and systems thinking."

Sources:

Jane Heifetz, Founder and Principal of Right Résumés — Harvard Business Review, July 28, 2015
Amanda Augustine, Career Advice Expert — TopResume, December 13, 2015

## Mirror the job description's language naturally

Rule: Pull the required and preferred terms from the target job description and use the same phrasing where it honestly matches your experience. Do this in the summary, skills section, and experience bullets.

Rationale: ATS systems and recruiters both screen for alignment. If your resume uses different words than the posting for the same capability, you can lose on relevance even when you are qualified.

Example violation: The posting calls for "incident management" and "stakeholder communication" but the resume only says "handled outages" and "worked with teams."

Example fix: "Led incident management during Sev 1 events and managed stakeholder communication across engineering, operations, and leadership."

Sources:

Jenny Foss, Job Search Strategist and Longtime Recruiter — The Muse, June 19, 2020
Heather Krasna, Author of The Muse guide for HR resumes — The Muse, October 13, 2021
Kelsey Purcell, CPRW — Jobscan, April 8, 2026

## Standard section headings beat clever branding

Rule: Use conventional headings like Experience, Skills, Education, Certifications, and Summary. Do not rename sections with cute labels.

Rationale: ATS parsers and recruiters both work faster with standard labels. Creative headings slow down human scanning and can break machine categorization.

Example violation: Sections labeled "Where I Have Been," "My Toolkit," and "Wins and Wisdom."

Example fix: Use "Experience," "Technical Skills," and "Education."

Sources:

Kelsey Purcell, CPRW — Jobscan, April 10, 2026
Amanda Augustine, Career Advice Expert — TopResume, June 3, 2025 (adds: standard labels improve ATS reliability)

## Keep critical data out of headers, footers, and text boxes

Rule: Do not put contact details, keywords, or important content inside document headers, footers, or text boxes.

Rationale: ATS tools often skip or scramble those regions. Hiding critical content there can cost you contactability and keyword coverage.

Example violation: Phone number, email, and LinkedIn URL placed in the Word header, with certifications inside a sidebar text box.

Example fix: Move all must read content into the main body at the top of page one.

## Single column wins over pretty layouts

Rule: Use a clean single column layout unless there is a compelling reason not to. Do not sacrifice readability or ATS parsing for visual style.

Rationale: Multi column layouts, graphics, icons, and flashy design elements often scramble reading order for ATS tools and slow down human reviewers.

Example violation: A two column resume with icons, charts, skill bars, and a sidebar containing tools, dates, and certifications.

Example fix: Convert to a single column layout with standard headings, plain text content, and selective use of bold for emphasis.

Sources:

Kelsey Purcell, CPRW — Jobscan, April 10, 2026
Jaclyn Westlake, former Agency Recruiter and HR Manager — The Muse, September 27, 2021
Amanda Augustine, Career Advice Expert — TopResume, June 3, 2025 (adds: simplicity beats visual cleverness for ATS compatibility)

## Your technical resume needs a clear narrative

Rule: A technical resume must do more than list tools. It should tell a clear story about what kind of builder, operator, or leader you are.

Rationale: Technical candidates often overload the page with stack names and under explain their value. Hiring managers want to know the shape of your contribution, not just the nouns you touched.

Example violation: "Python, AWS, Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform, Linux, Git, Jenkins, Ansible" with no unifying story.

Example fix: "Platform engineer focused on building reliable cloud infrastructure and developer automation. Most effective in environments that need faster deployments, tighter observability, and reduced manual operations."

Sources:

Peenak Inamdar, Director of Engineering at The Muse — The Muse, September 27, 2021

## Chronology should be easy to follow

Rule: Default to reverse chronological structure unless there is a real reason to do otherwise. Make career progression easy to scan.

Rationale: Recruiters prefer straightforward chronology because it shows growth, stability, and recency without forcing them to reconstruct the timeline.

Example violation: A functional resume with skill categories first, minimal dates, and employers buried at the bottom.

Example fix: Present roles in reverse chronological order with clear dates, titles, employers, and bullets ordered by relevance.

Sources:

Use hybrid structure only when the timeline needs help

Rule: If you are changing careers or returning after a break, use a hybrid structure to emphasize transferable skills without hiding the timeline. Do not use a skills first layout just to dodge uncomfortable chronology.

Rationale: Career pivots and breaks can be framed well, but disguising them creates suspicion. The better move is to foreground relevant skills and still show the timeline honestly.

Example violation: A career changer hides employment dates and employers to avoid showing a recent move from retail into IT support.

Example fix: Use a summary and skills block to foreground support, troubleshooting, and ticketing strengths, then show the actual work history with clear dates.

The bullet formula is verb, work, outcome

Rule: Build bullets with a strong action verb, a concrete description of the work, and the outcome or achievement. Add numbers whenever they are available.

Rationale: This formula forces clarity and prevents bullets from collapsing into vague responsibility statements.

Example violation: "Worked on onboarding and compliance projects."

Example fix: "Designed onboarding workflows and compliance tracking for 3 business units, cutting new hire processing time by 28%."

Sources:

Heather Krasna, Author of The Muse guide for HR resumes — The Muse, October 13, 2021
Put the strongest proof first under each role

Rule: Within each role, order bullets by impact and relevance, not by chronology or by whatever came to mind first.

Rationale: Recruiters may only read the first one or two bullets under a role. Leading with weak setup bullets buries the evidence that could win the interview.

Example violation: First bullet says "Attended weekly stakeholder meetings," while the fourth bullet contains the actual major automation win.

Example fix: Move the automation win to the first bullet, follow with the next strongest evidence, and leave lower value maintenance tasks later or cut them.

Sources:

Heather Krasna, Author of The Muse guide for HR resumes — The Muse, October 13, 2021
Skills lists need proof, not just nouns

Rule: Group skills by category and make sure the important ones also appear in the experience section. A skills block without proof reads like aspiration.

Rationale: Recruiters use the skills section for fast scanning, but hiring managers look for evidence. Repeating critical tools in both the skills block and relevant bullets makes the claim believable and ATS friendly.

Example violation: "AWS, Terraform, Kubernetes, CI/CD, Python, Linux, Docker, Grafana, Prometheus" listed with no matching bullets anywhere in experience.

Example fix: Group skills as Cloud, IaC, Containers, Observability, and Scripting, then reinforce the most important ones in bullets that show where and how you used them.

When hard metrics are missing, show scope instead of fluff

Rule: If you do not have exact numbers, do not invent them. Instead, make the bullet stronger by showing scope, complexity, ownership, stakeholders, frequency, or risk.

Rationale: Not every valuable contribution has a clean percentage attached. Scope based evidence is still stronger than generic claims, as long as it is truthful and concrete.

Example violation: "Improved cloud operations and communication."

Example fix: "Owned cloud operations during weekly release windows across 14 services, coordinating engineering, support, and security teams during high risk changes."

Explain career direction, do not make the recruiter guess

Rule: If your background is broad or non linear, use the summary and bullet emphasis to explain the direction of your candidacy. Do not expect the recruiter to connect the dots for you.

Rationale: Mixed backgrounds can look powerful or scattered depending on framing. The resume has to resolve that ambiguity quickly.

Example violation: A resume that mixes sales, operations, recruiting, and customer success work with no indication of the current target.

Example fix: "Operations leader with a recruiting and customer success foundation, now targeting people operations and workforce planning roles with heavy cross functional execution."

Simplicity signals judgment

Rule: Keep formatting simple, readable, and consistent. The resume is not the place to prove creativity with visual effects unless the role truly demands that and you still preserve scanability.

Rationale: Recruiters often read simplicity as professionalism and judgment. Overdesigned resumes create friction and can look like candidates are compensating for weak substance.

Example violation: Three fonts, multiple accent colors, icons for every section, and inconsistent bolding across titles and employers.

Example fix: One font family, restrained emphasis, consistent bullet formatting, and enough white space to make scanning easy.

The file name is part of the first impression

Rule: Name the resume file professionally and specifically. Use your name and the target role or a clean equivalent. Do not submit files named like drafts.

Rationale: Recruiters and hiring managers often see the file name before they read the document. Sloppy naming creates a careless first impression before your content even has a chance.

Example violation: resume_final_v7_REAL.pdf or Resume(1).docx

Example fix: FirstName_LastName_SeniorPlatformEngineer.pdf

Early career candidates should emphasize proof of readiness, not apologize for lack of years

Rule: If you are early career, replace generic eagerness with proof of readiness through projects, internships, coursework, tools used, and outcomes achieved.

Rationale: Junior candidates often waste space explaining that they are motivated. Recruiters already assume you want the job. What they need is evidence that you can contribute.

Example violation: "Motivated recent graduate eager to learn and contribute to a dynamic organization."

Example fix: "Recent computer science graduate with internship experience automating QA checks in Python, shipping internal tools used by 20 analysts, and maintaining CI workflows in GitHub Actions."

Sources:

Paige Cohen, Senior Editor — Harvard Business Review, May 23, 2022
Jane Heifetz, Founder and Principal of Right Résumés — Harvard Business Review, July 28, 2015
Senior candidates must show scope, not just tenure

Rule: Senior and executive candidates should emphasize scale, ownership, business impact, and cross functional influence. Years alone do not prove seniority.

Rationale: At senior levels, recruiters are not impressed by "20 years of experience" without evidence of complexity, decision making, and organizational effect.

Example violation: "Seasoned IT leader with over 20 years of experience in technology."

Example fix: "Technology leader who owned cloud modernization, security governance, and automation strategy across a 6 country environment, reducing manual operations costs while improving release reliability."

Proofread like a hiring manager is looking for a reason to say no

Rule: Proofread manually, not just with spellcheck. Then get another human to review it.

Rationale: Resume errors are disproportionately damaging because they create doubt about attention to detail, judgment, and professionalism. In close decisions, sloppy resumes lose.

Example violation: Mixed tenses, inconsistent punctuation, a company name spelled two different ways, and one bullet missing a verb.

Example fix: Review top to bottom, then bottom to top, standardize tense and formatting, and get external eyes on the final draft before submission.
